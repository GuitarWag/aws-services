§ 00 — Principal

What the exam is really testing

2 hours · ~50–60 questions · case studies included

The role

Translate business needs into a defensible architecture.

A Cloud Architect designs, plans, and manages Google Cloud solutions. You turn ambiguous business requirements into concrete technical decisions — and you justify them against security, reliability, scalability, cost, and operations.

The mindset

Judgment over memorization.

Most questions give a scenario and ask for the best answer, not the only correct one. Several options usually work; the exam rewards the one that fits the stated constraint — least privilege, lowest ops burden, meets the SLA, respects data residency.

The tension

Every decision is a trade-off.

Managed vs. control. Cost vs. performance. Global vs. regional. Portability vs. simplicity. Read what the scenario optimizes for, then pick the answer that spends the least to satisfy it.

What the exam is really asking

“Given this business constraint, which Google Cloud design meets it with the least operational burden and the fewest ways to go wrong?”

§ 01 — Framework

Google Cloud Well-Architected

Six pillars. The exam expects you to recognize which pillar a scenario is stressing.

Operational · Security · Reliability · Performance · Cost · Sustainability

Pillar 01 Ops

Operational Excellence

Run, monitor, and improve systems that deliver business value. Automate toil, define SLOs, learn from incidents.

Exam answer: pick the managed path if it cuts ops burden without breaking a requirement.
Pillar 02 Security

Security, Privacy & Compliance

Protect data and workloads. Least privilege, defense in depth, encryption everywhere, auditability, data residency.

Exam answer: the option that improves security and simplicity at once usually wins.
Pillar 03 Reliability

Reliability

Design for failure and recovery, not just normal operation. Redundancy, health checks, graceful degradation, tested DR.

Exam answer: know your RTO/RPO, then match the cheapest topology that meets them.
Pillar 04 Performance

Performance Optimization

Right-size resources to the workload. Autoscale, cache, place data near compute, benchmark before you tune.

Exam answer: measure first — scale the actual bottleneck, not the whole stack.
Pillar 05 Cost

Cost Optimization

Pay for what delivers value. Committed-use & spot discounts, autoscaling to zero, storage class tiering, budgets & alerts.

Exam answer: CUDs for steady load, Spot for fault-tolerant batch, autoscale for spiky.
Pillar 06 Sustainability

Sustainability

Reduce the carbon footprint of your architecture. Choose low-carbon regions, use efficient managed services, delete idle resources.

Exam answer: pick low-CO₂ regions when latency and residency allow it.
§ 02 — Exam Domains

Six domains, weighted

Where the questions come from — and what each one is really probing.

Weights are approximate; the guide lists them as objectives, not fixed counts.

Domain 1 ~25%

Designing & planning a cloud solution architecture

The biggest slice. Turn business & technical requirements into an architecture: HA, DR, network topology, storage/compute selection, and cost modeling.

  • Map requirements → services; justify against SLA/SLO, RTO/RPO.
  • Multi-region vs. regional; global load balancing; data locality.
  • Migration & hybrid planning; success metrics (KPIs).
Expect: long case-study scenarios with a stated constraint.
Domain 2 ~17.5%

Managing & provisioning solution infrastructure

Configure networks, storage, and compute to the design. Provision with IaC, not clicks.

  • VPC, subnets, firewall, hybrid links; Shared VPC / peering.
  • Terraform / Config Controller; deployment repeatability.
  • Data storage provisioning & compute configuration.
Expect: "how would you provision / configure X" questions.
Domain 3 ~17.5%

Designing for security & compliance

IAM, data protection, and meeting regulatory requirements by design.

  • Least-privilege IAM; resource hierarchy & Org Policy.
  • Encryption (CMEK/CSEK), KMS, Secret Manager, VPC-SC.
  • Compliance: residency, sovereignty, audit logging, SoD.
Expect: "which change makes this safer" questions.
Domain 4 ~15%

Analyzing & optimizing technical & business processes

Improve what exists. Cost, performance, team process, and continuous improvement.

  • Cost optimization; right-sizing; commitment discounts.
  • Software-development lifecycle & CI/CD improvements.
  • Analyzing & defining business processes and stakeholder needs.
Expect: "what would you optimize / change" questions.
Domain 5 ~12.5%

Managing implementation

From design to running system. Tooling, APIs, and working with dev & ops teams.

  • gcloud / bq / gsutil, Cloud SDK, Cloud Shell, client libraries.
  • API management with Apigee; programmatic interaction.
  • Advising dev/ops teams to keep implementation on-track.
Expect: "which tool / API / SDK" questions.
Domain 6 ~12.5%

Ensuring solution & operations reliability

Keep it running well. Monitoring, alerting, release management, and support.

  • Cloud Monitoring/Logging; SLIs, SLOs, error budgets.
  • Deployment & release management; rollback strategy.
  • Supportability, quality control, and incident response.
Expect: "how do you observe / recover / release" questions.
§ 03 — IAM & Governance

Identity, roles & the hierarchy

Who can do what, on which resource — and how policy flows down the tree.

Cloud IAM · Cloud Identity · Org Policy

A — Principals & core objects
№ 01 Identity

Google account / Cloud Identity

A human user

A person authenticated by Google. Cloud Identity (or Workspace) is the IdP that manages users & groups. Federate from your existing IdP where possible; don't create shadow accounts.

user:alice@example.com
№ 02 Grouping

Group

Grant to groups, not people

A named collection of users (and other groups). Bind roles to groups so access follows team membership — add/remove people in the IdP, never re-edit policies.

group:data-eng@example.com
№ 03 Workload

Service account

An identity for code, not a person

Used by apps/VMs to call APIs. Prefer attached service accounts + short-lived tokens. Avoid exporting SA keys — they're the #1 credential-leak risk.

svc@proj.iam.gserviceaccount.com
№ 04 Permissions

Role

A bundle of permissions

A role holds many permissions (service.resource.verb). Three kinds: Basic (Owner/Editor/Viewer — avoid), Predefined (per-service, use these), Custom (least-privilege, you curate).

roles/storage.objectViewer
№ 05 Binding

IAM policy binding

member + role, on a resource

An allow-policy is a set of bindings: this member has this role on this resource. There's no "deny by default rule" to write — GCP is additive: no grant = no access. IAM Deny policies add explicit deny on top.

{member, role} → resource
№ 06 Delegation

SA impersonation

Borrow an identity, briefly

Grant roles/iam.serviceAccountTokenCreator so a user or SA can mint short-lived tokens for another SA. Replaces long-lived keys and gives a clean audit trail.

generateAccessToken · TokenCreator

Access is additive — there is no implicit grant. If no binding gives a member a role with the needed permission, the request is denied. An IAM Deny policy overrides matching allows.

C — Governance guardrails

Organization Policy

Central constraints on how resources can be configured — e.g. restrict regions, block external IPs, enforce OS Login, disable SA key creation. Preventive guardrails, not per-user permissions.

Workload Identity Federation

Let external workloads (AWS, Azure, on-prem, GitHub Actions, K8s) impersonate an SA using their own tokens — no exported keys. The exam's preferred answer for external/CI access to GCP.

Policy Analyzer / Recommender

IAM Recommender flags over-granted roles and proposes tighter ones. Policy Analyzer answers "who can access what." Use them to walk Basic roles down to least privilege.

Cloud Audit Logs

Admin Activity logs are on & free. Enable Data Access logs for sensitive reads. Sink to a locked bucket / BigQuery for tamper-evident, long-retention audit trails.

Do — least privilege by default
  • Grant predefined or custom roles, never Owner/Editor in prod.
  • Bind to groups; manage membership in the IdP.
  • Grant at the lowest level that works (project > folder > org).
  • Use impersonation / WIF instead of SA keys.
  • Separate duties — different SAs per app & environment.
Avoid — the classic exam traps
  • Basic Owner/Editor at the org or project level.
  • Downloaded, long-lived, unrotated SA keys.
  • Granting roles to individual users instead of groups.
  • Using the default compute SA with broad scopes.
  • Over-scoping "just to make it work," then never trimming.

§ 04 — Networking

How to think about VPC design

The exam frames networking as reachability & isolation decisions, not a features tour.

VPC is global · subnets are regional

The one thing to remember

A GCP VPC is global; its subnets are regional. One VPC can span every region without VPN or peering — routing is built in. That reshapes most "how do I connect these" answers versus AWS.

Isolation

VPC & subnets

A VPC is a global private network; subnets carve out regional IP ranges. Use custom-mode VPCs (not auto) in production so you control CIDR layout and avoid overlaps with on-prem/other clouds.

Filtering

Firewall rules & tags

Stateful allow/deny rules scoped by network tags or service accounts — prefer SA-based targeting over tags. Default-deny ingress; open only what's needed. Hierarchical firewall policies apply org/folder-wide.

Routing

Routes & Cloud NAT

Routes steer traffic; Cloud Router advertises via BGP for hybrid. Give VMs no external IP and use Cloud NAT for outbound-only internet — a common "make it more secure" answer.

Sharing

Shared VPC

A host project owns the network; service projects attach to it. Central network/security team controls subnets & firewall while app teams deploy into them. The answer for org-wide, centrally-governed networking.

Connecting

VPC Peering vs. PSC

Peering joins two VPCs' route tables (non-transitive). Private Service Connect exposes a specific service via a private endpoint — no broad network join, no IP overlap worries. Prefer PSC for consuming managed/partner services privately.

Private access

Private Google Access

Lets internal-only VMs reach Google APIs (Storage, BigQuery) without external IPs. Pair with VPC Service Controls to stop data from leaving your perimeter.

Load balancing — match the LB to the traffic

Hybrid & multicloud — how the exam frames the choice

Cloud VPN

IPsec over the public internet. Cheap, fast to stand up. HA VPN gives 99.99% SLA. Choose when bandwidth is modest and cost matters.

Dedicated Interconnect

Private physical link, 10/100 Gbps. Choose for high, steady bandwidth and consistent low latency to on-prem. Partner Interconnect for sub-10G via a provider.

Cross-Cloud Interconnect

Dedicated private link to AWS/Azure. Choose for real multicloud data planes instead of routing everything over VPN.

§ 06 — Security & Compliance

What makes an answer defensible

Defense in depth: identity, keys, secrets, perimeter, and proof.

Encrypt everywhere · least privilege · prove it

Keys

Cloud KMS · CMEK · CSEK

Data is always encrypted at rest by Google. For control, use CMEK (your keys in KMS) — required when compliance says you must hold the keys. CSEK / External Key Manager go further: keys you supply or keep off-cloud.

Secrets

Secret Manager

Store API keys, passwords, certs with versioning, IAM-scoped access, and rotation. The correct answer whenever a scenario has secrets hard-coded in config, images, or env vars.

Perimeter

VPC Service Controls

Draws a service perimeter around managed APIs (Storage, BigQuery) so data can't be exfiltrated to projects outside it — even with valid credentials. The answer for "prevent data exfiltration" & residency.

Access

IAP & Context-Aware Access

Identity-Aware Proxy fronts apps & SSH/RDP with identity checks — no VPN, no public IPs. Context-Aware Access adds device & location conditions. The BeyondCorp / zero-trust answer for secure remote access.

Threats

SCC · Cloud Armor

Security Command Center surfaces misconfigs, vulnerabilities, and threats org-wide. Cloud Armor gives WAF rules & DDoS protection at the global load balancer edge.

Supply chain

Binary Authorization

Only lets attested, signed images deploy to GKE/Cloud Run. Pair with Artifact Registry scanning & SLSA provenance for software supply-chain security.

Data residency & sovereignty

When a scenario says data must stay in a country/region: pin resources to that region, use Org Policy to restrict resource locations, wrap sensitive APIs in VPC Service Controls, and consider CMEK/EKM so key custody stays with you. Sovereign-cloud & Assured Workloads options exist for strict regulatory regimes.

PII & regulated / secure-AI workloads

Use Sensitive Data Protection (DLP) to discover, classify, and de-identify PII before it lands in analytics. For AI, keep training data inside the perimeter, use CMEK on model & data stores, ground models with your own governed data, and log access — the guide expects security to extend to Gemini/Vertex AI workloads too.

Rule of thumb

If two answers both work, the safer one usually wins: no public IP, no exported key, scoped IAM, encryption you control, and an audit trail that proves what happened.

§ 07 — Operations & Reliability

Observe, alert, recover

Observability is part of the design, not an afterthought. A recovery plan matters as much as a deploy plan.

Cloud Operations Suite · SRE practices

01

Observe

Cloud Monitoring (metrics), Cloud Logging (logs), Cloud Trace (latency), Cloud Profiler (CPU/heap). Instrument before you launch, not after the outage.

02

Alert

Define SLIs → set SLOs → alert on the error budget burn, not on raw CPU. Page humans only for user-facing symptoms; route the rest to dashboards.

03

Recover

Know your RTO/RPO. Automated backups, multi-region replicas, tested restores. A backup you've never restored is a hope, not a plan.

04

Release safely

Cloud Deploy / Cloud Build pipelines with canary & blue-green rollouts and instant rollback. Ship small, ship often, keep a way back.

05

Test failure

Load-test to find limits; inject failure (chaos) to prove redundancy works. Design for graceful degradation, not just the happy path.

§ 08 — Migration & Implementation

From design to a running system

Plan the move, map dependencies, provision with code, and interact programmatically.

Migration Center · Terraform · Cloud SDK

A — Migration strategy — pick per workload, not per portfolio
Lift & shift

Rehost

Move VMs as-is with Migrate to Virtual Machines. Fastest path; least optimization. Good when the deadline is the constraint.

Tweak

Replatform

Small changes for big wins — e.g. self-managed MySQL → Cloud SQL, or containerize onto GKE. "Move & improve."

Rebuild

Refactor

Re-architect to cloud-native (serverless, managed data). Highest long-term payoff; highest upfront effort & risk.

Plan & assess

Migration Center & dependency mapping

Discover the existing estate, map application dependencies, right-size targets, and model TCO before you move anything. Migration follows four phases: Assess → Plan → Migrate → Optimize. Account for licensing (BYOL vs. pay-as-you-go) and test strategy up front.

Provision as code

Terraform & IaC

Terraform is the default IaC answer on GCP — declarative, repeatable, version-controlled, reviewable. Infrastructure Manager runs it as a managed service; Config Controller enforces desired state on GKE. Never "click-ops" production.

§ 09 — Study Plan

A six-week run-up

Adapted from the cohort learning plan into an actionable roadmap. Build breadth first, then depth, then judgment.

Foundations → Depth → Design & readiness

Phase 1 Week 1

Foundations

  • Core infrastructure — projects, resource hierarchy, IAM.
  • Security fundamentals & the shared-responsibility model.
  • App-dev environment: Cloud Shell, gcloud, Cloud SDK.
  • Networking basics & load balancing.
Goal: get comfortable moving around the console & CLI.
Phase 2 Weeks 2–3

Depth & patterns

  • Networking in depth: VPC, Shared VPC, hybrid connectivity.
  • Scaling & autoscaling; architecture building blocks.
  • Managing security: IAM depth, Org Policy, KMS.
  • Start working sample exam questions early.
Goal: connect services into repeatable patterns.
Phase 3 Weeks 4–6

Design & readiness

  • Observability, GKE, Cloud Run, Terraform hands-on.
  • Architecture design & process; AI/ADK modules.
  • Full case-study practice under time pressure.
  • Exam-readiness review & targeted revision.
Goal: practice judgment, not recall.
Review cadence

The cohort ran 6 review sessions across the program — use recurring checkpoints to consolidate, not one final cram.

Support

Technical support & community ran through the whole cohort period — treat peers & mentors as a first-class resource.

Voucher gate

The exam voucher depended on completing at least 5 of 7 skill badges — hands-on labs are the deliverable, not optional extras.

Final phase

Exam-readiness & revision are their own phase — book the exam only once mock case studies feel routine.
